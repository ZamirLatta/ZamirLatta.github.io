Zamir Latta Mr. Burns English 10 October 7, 2022 < object align: "center" (“"Four Walls"”) It was a warm, ordinary Friday when it happened. The sun rose, birds chirped, and the aftermath of July 4th still rang throughout the neighborhood. Of course I wasn’t paying any mind to it. A bunch of sweaty old men signed a big paper? Big deal. I thought this as I sat in my darkened, frigid, basement, as it would often get in the afternoon. The air smelled of aging cement and the floor displayed a complicated and glazed over brown galaxy within the epoxy. And as I sat on the white rug, I patiently but restlessly waited for the YouTube video I was watching to end. I needed to do something else, but I was pulled so far into my TV that it was impossible to crawl out. Yet again, procrastination was my own undoing. How typical. But all of a sudden, alone in this cooled basement, the cold confusion that usually populated the quiet walls was overcome with a warmth. I paid no mind. My head started getting heavy. My body felt like the supper being cooked by fiery coals. My brain was spinning. The world seemed like it was collapsing; my skull felt like it was being pulled apart from one ear to another. My heart raced. My heart raced again. It was the end of the world! All would end! But then… a rush of relief waved over my head. There’s no way that the world would end right now. Occam’s Razor pointed to my new hypothesis. It’s probably nothing anyway, I thought as I went back to staring at my screen. Anything and everything went back to its own mundane and boring way. Everyone lived ‘happily ever after.’ Except that didn’t happen. In fact, things got a lot worse. The coals burned with more fury. My head felt heavier. My brain ripped itself into further shreds of confetti. But I dismissed it again. I’m just tired. I’m just hungry. Whatever the cause of this pain was, I never thought it would become the very thing it did - the thing that kept me locked away in my room for almost a week. That was only a couple of hours before I found out that I had Covid. That was only a couple of hours before my life became confined to a cube. That was only a couple of hours before my life screeched to a halt. Only a couple of hours before my epiphany. After my dinner, as I laid perfectly but painfully still, I blamed myself for my own miserable feelings. “How could you get Covid?” the voice waffled. “How weak could your immune system even be? Have you been the person who hasn't washed their hands and been licking doorknobs all day? You must be the knob.” “It wasn’t my fault,” I replied back in my head. “My brother got Covid on Tuesday.” “Right. You still could have avoided it,” the voice replied back confidently. I could never explain why, but these meaningless arguments, these unrelated questions, these terrible points, and those stupid remarks always got their way. Waffling on and about, the voice held its own, no matter the occasion. Over and over, the voice entertained itself all night with conspiracy theories and more ways to give me insomnia. With my head still throbbing, I attempted to go to bed early, tired from dealing with my issues. As I sat in bed, I fumed with the bitterness of black coffee, under the covers in my cold and dark room. The next few days went by smoothly - except Tuesday. Although I had enjoyed my days off on the weekend, I was actually going to miss my summer job due to getting Covid, which set off a reaction cocktail: a shot of sadness, a drop of dread, a dash of displeasure, a splash of storminess. Now I was actually losing money. I hated myself for this. This was it. It’s probably my own fault I got Covid. Like a tug of war, I was being pulled from both sides of my own thoughts. This way. That way. Right. Left. Forward. Backward. Go here. Go there. Here. There. Anywhere, everywhere. A headache. But it was different from the knocking from Covid. It was a different kind of virus. Yet both still made me want to vomit. Can you believe it? I actually had to call my job and tell them I wasn’t going to make it. This is so embarrassing, I thought to myself as I dialed the number. “You’re going to screw it up,” the voice called. “No I’m not. It’ll be fine,” I said nervously back to myself. The voice had gripped me with its large, smog-coloured, bony hands. I could feel the grip tightening around my chest, squeezing the breath from out of my lungs. My own creation to help was choking me. Yet, I still had to make the call. This could make me lose my job. My heart beat faster and faster. An overture of thoughts raced through the quivering neurons down my spine. Deep bass vibrated through my hands reaching for the phone. A cascading crescendo of brass clogged my thought process. Cellos and violins arco over measures with sudden sharps. I shakily picked up the phone and told them my predicament. They said it was okay. It was so sweet, yet bitter. I overreacted again. I was an alarmist again. I exaggerated again. I was saddened again. As afternoon rolled around, restlessness was the word of the hour. What if I wasn’t better the next day? What if I lost out on even more days of work? The very thought of it was terrifying. Sure, I was having a day off. But I didn’t want it. I collapsed on my bed, exhausted from all of my own questions. The questions that cut deeply like a knife to the stomach. I was done with all of the drama. But it still kept coming. Since this was an extremely rare occasion, I rarely ever got to sit down with myself, let alone ever really ponder. As I was lying down, I looked around at the tan drywall surrounding me. Who am I really? “A sick child who’s losing lots of money,” the voice suggested conceitedly. “That’s not right-” I started. “Crippled by anxiety and weakened by your own self-hate? You disgust me.” “Okay, hold on-” “Why don’t you just grow up? Try being tough for a change,” the voice snarked. The bitterness was back. But this time, I wouldn’t let the voice win. I wasn’t going to succumb to the voice’s squabbles any longer. “What are you going to do?” the voice questioned impatiently. I stood up. “There’s no way you’re right,” I replied. “How? I’m always right. There’s no endgame for you here,” the voice started. “Maybe in your opinion,” I said. “But I’m out of patience for your stupid opinions.” “You can’t even escape your own mental cube. I don’t think you have the guts to stray out of the box.” “I already have.” “Like you’re attracted to both sexes and an atheist. How could you possibly sustain this ‘new identity’? I bet it’s just a phase,” the voice said as it puffed its chest. “It’s not a phase. And it never will be,” I said anxiously, fearing that I was standing on unstable ground. The grip was squeezing tighter. I was being asphyxiated. I braced for the finishing blow that would end me. The final stab that makes me bleed out my guts. The final bullet that stops my pulse. The final blow that sucks the life out of my soul and shows me the wrath of death on the horizon. I tensed, but nothing happened. My lungs couldn’t feel a grip anymore. A weight had been lifted off of my chest and my eyes regained color. The voice’s recognizable echo evaporated. A rush went over my head, which was peacefully resting on my pillow. The plaguing virus I was dealing with was fought off for now. I breathed a sigh of relief and slept knowing that I at least fought off one disease from my life for now. From that point on, I knew the voice would disappear one day.
